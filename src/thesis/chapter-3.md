## System Aesthetic

Burnham's key argument was that the most important art of his time was not concerned with the production of objects but with the production of systems. A system aesthetic is not the aesthetics of a particular style or form, but the aesthetics of systems as such — the beauty of a system well-made, a system that works, a system that reveals something true about the world.

> Sculpture and painting are making it impossible for modernist critics to maintain their domain.[^10]

## A Transition From an Object-Oriented to a System-Oriented Culture

The shift Burnham saw was happening everywhere in culture. In architecture, urban planning, music, and art, the emphasis was moving from the individual object to the system, from the single thing to the relationships between things. This is the transition from modernism — which celebrated the autonomous object — to something new.

Dan Graham's *Homes for America* (1966–67) is a good example of this shift. Rather than making an object to hang on a gallery wall, Graham documented the system of suburban housing developments — the grid, the repetition, the logic of mass production applied to the home. The artwork is the system, not any single house.

## Art Does Not Reside in Material Entities

If art is fundamentally about systems and relationships, then it cannot be located in any single material object. The artwork is not the painting on the wall but the total system: the artist, the context, the viewer, the institution, the social relations. Hans Haacke's *Condensation Cube* (1963–65) makes this literal: the work is a closed system — a sealed acrylic cube in which water condenses and evaporates according to the temperature of the gallery space and the breath of visitors. The artwork is the process, not the object.

## Art is Not Autonomous

One of the key claims of Systems Aesthetics is that art is not and cannot be autonomous. Art is always embedded in a context, always part of a larger system of relations. To claim autonomy for art is to misunderstand what art is. Martha Rosler's photomontages make this point forcefully: by embedding images of the Vietnam War into the domestic interiors of *House Beautiful*, she shows that the domestic and the political are part of the same system.

## Art is Conceptual Focus

What art provides is a specific kind of attention, a way of focusing on particular systems and making them visible. The artwork is the lens through which we look at the system, not the system itself. Mel Bochner's *Measurement Room* (1969) does exactly this: by writing the dimensions of a room directly on its walls, he makes visible the system of measurement that is always already there but usually invisible.

## No Definition or Theory of Art Can Be Historically Unvarying

Because art is always embedded in its context and because contexts change, no single definition of art can be valid for all times. Art must be understood historically. What counts as art changes as the systems in which art operates change. Burnham was acutely aware of this: his own essay was itself a product of its moment, the late 1960s, a moment of great change in art, in politics, in culture.

## Timeframe

Burnham was writing at a specific moment, and his claims need to be understood in relation to that moment. The 1960s were a period of enormous expansion in the definition of art. Happenings, Performance Art, Land Art, Conceptual Art, Arte Povera — all of these movements were questioning the traditional art object. Burnham was trying to find a theoretical framework that could account for all of these developments, and Systems Theory gave him that framework.

## Approach

The approach I take in my work is informed by all of these ideas. I am interested in systems, in the relations between things, in how order is made and unmade. I do not believe in the autonomous artwork, but in the artwork as part of a larger system of relations. When I look at Robert Smithson's work, I see this too: the Spiral Jetty is a system, a feedback loop between the artwork, the landscape, time, and the viewer.
