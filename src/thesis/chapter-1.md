> In the last resort, however, it is always a system of values, of ideas, of ideologies — choose whatever word you like — that is decisive.
>
> — Ludwig von Bertalanffy[^5]

It seems that rules play an important part in the way I work. When I start a work, I usually start with a question, a fascination or a problem. In most cases the solution brings another fixed set of rules in which I work.

## Origin

The text I'm using, *General System Theory*, is from 1968 and is a collection of papers written by Ludwig von Bertalanffy in the years before. The book has had a great influence in many scientific areas. The influence stretched further than just the sciences. It was used in management, social sciences and so on. Even though this text is quite old, I found the ideas still very relevant.

The main idea of General Systems Theory was to create an all-encompassing science that could cross between different disciplines. The idea is that the same kind of logical and mathematical laws apply for systems regardless of the domain of investigation. By seeking these similarities it can also show structural differences and make it possible to see where something from one domain can transfer to another domain and what might be the problems of such a transfer.

## Definition of System

> A system can be defined as a set of objects together with relationships between the objects and between their attributes.[^6]

Hall and Fagen offer this definition which Ludwig von Bertalanffy adopted in his book. A system consists of objects and the relations between them. The objects can have different attributes. An important element in the definition is that there are relations. There are always at least two elements in a relation. A single object is not yet a system.

General Systems Theory is a science that studies the general concept of systems and aims to explain rules and principles that are valid for all types of systems. The aim is to show structural similarities in the different sciences.

## Environment

> Every system has an environment, the set of all objects a change in whose attributes affect the system and also those objects whose attributes are changed by the behaviour of the system.[^7]

The environment then consists of all elements that are not part of the system but do have influence on it or are influenced by it. The environment and the system are always linked to each other. When you change the environment you also change the system and vice versa.

## Subsystems

Within every system there are subsets that can be seen as systems in themselves: subsystems. These can overlap. A subsystem can interact with the environment of the larger system. Everything can be seen as a subsystem of a larger system.

## Two Kinds of Systems

An important distinction within systems theory is the one between open and closed systems.

> Closed systems are isolated from the environment. Open systems exchange matter with the environment, exhibit import and export, building-up and breaking-down of their material components.[^8]

Most biological systems are open systems. They are in constant exchange with their environment, taking in energy, nutrients and matter, and releasing waste products. A closed system, by contrast, has no exchange with its environment. Eventually a closed system will reach a state of equilibrium, a state of maximum entropy.

## Compatibility

The notion of compatibility is used to describe how systems relate to each other. It addresses whether two or more systems can work together without conflict. In my work, I am often interested in what happens when two incompatible systems are brought together, or when a system is forced to operate in an environment for which it was not designed.

## Assessing

What I find most useful in General Systems Theory is the idea that the same structural principles can apply across completely different domains. The theory gives me permission to make connections across disciplines, to see the same pattern in a scientific text and in a work of art, in a biological process and in a social structure. This is exactly what I do when I make work: I find a structure or a system in one domain and transpose it to another.
