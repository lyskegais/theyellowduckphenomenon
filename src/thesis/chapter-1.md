> In the last resort, however it is always a system of values of ideas, of ideologies - choose whatever word you like - that is decisive
>
> — Ludwig von Bertalanffy

It seems that rules play an important role in my work. In a way you can see a set of rules as a system. The rules work together, influence each other and give a certain outcome. Because of this I will look at systems. How do systems work, what kind of systems are there? Can I relate these back to art and back to my work?

## Origin

Ludwig von Bertalanffy (1901-1972) was an Austrian theoretical biologist. When he was 17 he started to study history of art and philosophy and after eight years he finished his PhD. Two years later, in 1928, he published his first book on theoretical biology. In the 1930s he developed the organismic system theory that would form the basis for his General Systems Theory. A main feature that he finds in organic systems and uses in his General Systems Theory is the self-regulative capability. In an open system, that is a system continuously interacting with its environment, there is a mechanism that will try to get the system to a steady state in which there is a minimum of entropy and, hence, a minimum loss of energy. The striving towards this state gives the structure of the system stability

Bertalanffy brings the concept of the General Systems Theory as one that can be applied in all sciences, not only to, for example, the biology (where he started). It then becomes possible to transfer data from one science to another. This has pushed the study of system theories a lot further

But instead of using Bertalanffy’s book to understand this theory, I’ll be taking a text that originates at Bell Telephone Laboratories. It is a summary of the systems theory based, among others, on the text by Bertalanffy. Bell Laboratories, originally founded by the inventor of the telephone Alexander Graham Bell, was the research and development section of AT&T, the American Telephone & Telegraph Company and Western Electric. A small group devoted to basic research was particularly successful. It produced several Nobel prize winners and was the place where Claude Shannon wrote his paper ‘A Mathematical Theory of Communication’, the basis for the information theory. The information theory also exerted a big influence on art in the late 60s and early 70s

A.D. Hall and R.E. Fagen wrote an introductory text in which they discussed the properties of systems in an abstract way. The text was initially written for a course at Bell Telephone Laboratories in New York but was later revised and reprinted in the Yearbook of the Society for the Advancement of General Systems Theory. The society was founded under this name in 1954 by Ludwig von Bertalanffy, Kenneth Boulding, Ralph Gerard and Anatol Rapoport but was generally called Society for General Systems Research (SGSR). It still exists now as the International Society for the Systems Sciences (ISSS). But in all their records A.D. Hall and R.E. Fagen are not to be found. Whilst their text is used in a vast amount of articles and books on systems and considered a classic, it is not clear who they were exactly

## Definition of System

In ‘Definition of System’ Hall and Fagen explain in a very clear way the theory of Bertalanffy. It starts with a very general definition of system and then defines the elements and their properties

A system is a set of objects together with relationships between the objects and between their attributes

So a system consists of objects. These objects have attributes and between both there are relationships. Objects are the parts or components of the system, the attributes properties of the objects and the relationships between them is what ties the system together

In my research for a work I’m looking for relationships between different parts of the subject. These relationships are the key. They are what make systems relevant to my art work. Usually I become fascinated by a topic and start to look in as many directions as I can that are related to it. Between all these side roads, there are from time to time links, similarities. Those things usually end up being important for my work. So actually I’m wondering if you can reason the other way round. If I use in my art the objects that have relationships with other objects (but both from different roads)

Is my art a system? And more generally, do all groups of objects that have attributes with relationships make a system?

It is possible to find between any two objects a relationship, how trivial it may be, but a relationship nontheless. What determines if a relationship is useful or useless is the question that is asked at that particular moment. Changing the question means changing the relevant relationships. The person asking decides the question itself. So systems, how objective and true they might sound or may look on the surface, are at the very beginning totally dependent on the subjective interest of the person asking the question

So far it is still possible to apply that to art. When making a work of art, it is the artist who decides what question he is asking, or what the subject is. In that sense you could say that the artist is creating a system

Apart from relationships between objects within a system, there are also parts in or between systems that are identical. So maybe finding these similarities is even more important than relationship when considering my art work. The systems theory talks about isomorphism, something which is structurally identical. Especially in mathematical systems and when you look at systems at an abstract level, this is very often the case. This property can be very handy when studying systems and using one system to apply to another

## Environment

Another element of systems is environment. The definition given by Hall and Fagen for it is:

For a given system, the environment is the set of all objects a change in whose attributes affect the system and also those objects whose attributes are changed by the behavior of the system

The combination of the objects in the environment and the objects that make the system is called the universe. This universe thus consists of questions that define the system and the environment. These questions are still subjective, as is again dividing the objects of the universe into system or environment. The dividing of objects is a dichotomization, since every object must be either in the system or in the environment. Further, what is in the system can’t be in the environment and visa versa. Here the comparison with art doesn’t work. You can indeed divide the art in what the art work is and what the public is, but there are lots of works where the public becomes part of the artwork or is asked to participate. It isn’t that the public then becomes just part of the art. It is still also a viewer, and in that sense part of the environment. So here I can say that art is not a system, since its components can be part of the system as well part of the environment

For sciences like physics and chemistry the division of objects is possible and the abstraction will even simplify things. But for fields like biology, sociology and psychology this is a lot more complex. How do these disciplinary fields solve this problem? How can it be applied to the field of arts?

## Subsystems

A tool for this could be to divide the system further into subsystems and give them a hierarchical order. The subsystems can be less complex and easier to understand, and understanding the subsystems might give insight into the main system. On the other hand, it could also provide a lot of information that is not relevant to the question at hand. And what makes this method even more complex is that objects that are part of one subsystem could be the environment for another subsystem. So here you can argue two ways. Creating subsystems will make you define the system more precisely. But it could also create an overload of information, making it easy to lose the general point. I think you should strive for precision and that in the details (subsystems) you can find the general point without having to be general. A detail of the bigger picture will always say something about this bigger picture but will avoid falling into meaningless generalization. A relevant point on this comes from the French naturalist Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon (1707-1788): ‘The more we increase the number of divisions in the productions of nature, the closer we shall approach to the true, since nothing really exists in nature, except individuals, and since genera, orders, and classes exist only in our imagination’. The subsystem is a term we put on the object, so when we go on dividing the system to the point it is at the core, we end up with the separate element. This element doesn’t have any of our interpretation on it, so it is indeed the most true. Maybe this is why they are searching so hard for the undividable particle at CERN

To have a macroscopic view on a system will still leave the problem of how to sort the objects. As the name implies, the ‘General Systems Theory’ will not be specific, but since a lot has been written about this subject, there are different classes of systems described. These classes have their own properties, and by looking at those properties and finding one that fits the specific question you can make useful connections. They can serve as a tool. The properties given by Hall and Fagen:

Wholeness (coherent) and independence (summative) is one property, a system is to a certain degree coherent (or not). To know what state a system is in helps with the next property. ‘Most nonabstract systems change with time. If these changes lead to a gradual transition from wholeness to summativity, the system is said to undergo progressive segregation.’ A process in which there is change towards wholeness is called progressive systematization. There is also progressive centralization, this is when a system moves towards a system where one part or subsystem plays a dominant role. A system like this is called a centralized system

## Two Kinds of Systems

Then there is the distinction between natural and man-made systems. The important difference between the two is that we have control over the systems we make (man-made). These are systems we understand and can unravel from beginning to end. Whilst natural systems are out of our reach. We are working to understand them, but we can never be sure if we know all there is about them. There is this mystical aspect. When we presume that my work is a system, it is a man-made system. But the inspiration and often the execution of my work lies in the physical world, the environment. This environment consists mostly of man-made systems and also of natural ones. Although I do have a personal interest in nature, it seems that when it comes to art my attention is mostly drawn to man-made things within a natural environment. The only work in which I’m really using a natural system is a concept for a floating neighbourhood in which the houses are close together when the tide is in and spread out when the tide is at its lower point. What is interesting to realize is that man-made systems are often based upon natural systems. The similarities and connections they have are a subject I want to explore more in my work

Then the last important properties of systems I want to point out. This starts with the fact that natural systems can be open or closed. Open systems exchange materials, energies or information with their environment, while closed systems don’t. When in exchange with the environment you can have an adaptive system. ‘They possess the ability to react to their environments in a way that is favourable, in some sense, to the continued operation of the system.’ This is closely related to stable systems in which the variables in the system stay within defined limits. For adaptive systems to function in afavourable way, some variables must remain within certain limits and thus are stable. A system can have stable and unstable parts at the same time. Further, natural systems can be systems with feedback when their output is fed back into the input of the system and has an effect again on the following output. This process influences the stability or instability of a system. An example of an artwork with feedback could be the work Milton Friedman on the Wonder of the Free Market Pencil, 41 language alphabetical Google translation loop, starting and finishing in English, 42 digital prints on paper, each 21 x 29.7cm; 2009 of Gareth Kennedy. I saw it in the Irish pavilion at the 53rd Venice Biennale in 2009. The artist took a text, translated this in Google, took this translation, translated it again and again and again. The act of translating the text in Google is the system. This system gives an output (the translated text), and this output is again put through the same system (but translated into a different language this time), thus giving a slightly different output again. Just note that his use of the system wasn’t the subject of the work. The system was a tool

The properties given for natural systems can be related back to art, starting with my opinion that art is always an open system. It is exchanging materials, energies or information with its environment, whether intended by the artist or not. With it being an open system it is important to realize that ‘as Bertalanffy noted in An Outline of General Systems Theory (1950), the complexity of open social systems is far greater than in closed physical systems, which makes their analysis more difficult.’ There are artworks that are static objects and aren’t changed by the audience. This is the majority, but there is also art that does respond to its environment and changes, I am thinking here of performances for instance. These can also be adaptive or work with feedback. Although the last two circumstances are rare I think

Almost all the properties ascribed to the natural system also apply to man-made systems. The main differences arise when you look at machines as man-made systems. Machines do not show any adaptive behaviour. Although since software is now regulating most machines, you could say they’ve started to acquire some adaptive qualities. But in my opinion you should separate the hardware (which I think is the actual machine) and the thing that operates it (the software, in this case a sort of brain)

Machines are one of the clearest examples of man-made systems. But also language, social organization and information belong to this group. And these systems have a lot of similarities with natural systems, including the adaptive quality

## Compatibility

Two more things to consider about the man-made system start with compatibility (or harmony). This has to do with how a system relates to its environment. This is especially important for man-made systems, which are made with a certain predefined environment in mind. The creator of the system sets the condition for how he wants the system to work and in what kind of environment. If the environment is changed, the system could very possibly stop functioning the way it was designed. Or as Hall and Fagen put it: ‘Systems might be compatible in some respects and incompatible in others; it depends on the purpose for which the systems are introduced as well as the environmental factors.’ I think this aspect is important because it again shows how much the environment influences the system. That you can’t separate the system from its environment. When making or considering a system you have to be aware of it. And I think this is also true for art. I think that when making an art piece you have to take into account the environment. Even if you don’t want it to play a role, excluding it is still a conscious decision. So whatever you do, you need to be aware of the environment in which you place your system or art

## Assessing

In the end we have to judge the success of a system. When does it function well and when does it not? This depends on how you look at it, since it could have different outcomes from different perspectives. So this leads to optimization, because you want the system to work optimally in the circumstances you have set for it. It seems that the success of a system is largely dependent on how well the question for it is put. If you are not clear about what you want from the system, or where you want it, it won’t function properly. Be clear about what you want is probably a universal property for a good result

An interesting part of systems is, I think, randomness. ‘In practice it is usually introduced as a factor when the variables that may affect a given attribute are so great in number or so inaccessible that there is no choice but to consider behavior as subject to chance.’ With art in mind, this randomness could be inspiration. The factor where you are not sure why it is, but it is in a certain way. In scientific systems they say that random is when there are too many options possible, so it becomes chance and you won’t know what will happen. So here it might not match with inspiration. Of course there are innumerable reasons for a certain inspiration, but when it’s there it is one very solid thought that you could call fixed. So I’m not sure if you could call randomness inspiration

Synthesis of systems is much more difficult. Here science and engineering begin to take on aspects of art. A systems designer or planner not only must construct systems that work harmoniously individually and in tandem, he must also know a lot about the environment that the system is intended to match. Consideration of environmental factors requires foresight and experience; no one can ever foresee all the variables of importance and a choice of which to include is often a difficult one to make. (…) In summary, a scientist in his analysis, evaluation and synthesis of systems is not concerned primarily with the pieces of hardware that make up a system, but with the concept of system as a whole; its internal relations, and its behaviour in the given environment

Up to now I’ve been following the theory of systems in a very rigid order and applied my thoughts for how it could relate to art, one to one. But things aren’t this simple. You can’t take a scientific text, apply it to art and make a universal statement out of it. Luckily already in 1968 Jack Burnham wrote the System Aesthetics text. In it, he applied the system theory to the post-formalist art movement

>
