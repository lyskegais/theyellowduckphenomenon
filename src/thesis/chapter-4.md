## Truth

The question of truth in art and in knowledge more broadly is central to my practice. I do not believe in a single truth, but in multiple truths that are always situated, always produced within specific contexts and relations. What is true within one system may not be true within another. This is not relativism — some truths are better, more precise, more useful than others — but it is a recognition that truth is always produced, always made.

## Unity

The idea of unity is related to the idea of a system. A system is unified, in the sense that it holds together as a whole, but it is not homogeneous. Unity in diversity: the system holds together precisely because of the differences between its elements, not despite them. This is what makes a system interesting — not the sameness of its parts but the specific way they relate to each other.

## System

The concept of system is what ties together my practice. When I make work, I set up a system — a set of rules, a framework, a structure — and then I follow it. The system is the artwork, not the physical result. The physical result is just the trace of the system at work.

This is why I keep coming back to von Bertalanffy and Burnham. They give me a language for talking about what I do. When I make *Stegen van Harlingen*, I am making a system: a set of rules for how to document a place, how to order the documentation, how to present the result. The bricks and the labels and the photographs are not the work. The system is the work.

## Aesthetic Enjoyment

The aesthetic dimension of the work is not separate from its conceptual dimension. The aesthetic experience is the experience of the system, the experience of the relations between things. When you encounter a work of mine, I want you to feel the system — to sense the structure, to see the order, and then to wonder what the order reveals.

There is pleasure in finding order in apparent chaos. There is also pleasure in finding chaos in apparent order. Both are aesthetic experiences, and both are experiences of systems.

## Inspiration

Where does inspiration come from? For me, it comes from observation, from looking at the world and finding structures and systems in what I see. The world is full of systems, and making art is a way of making some of those systems visible. I am not inventing systems; I am finding them and making them visible.

## Perception

Perception is always structured. We do not see raw data but always already interpreted information, ordered and categorised by our perceptual and cognitive systems. What we see is a product of the systems through which we see. This means that the act of perception is always already an act of ordering.

Making art, for me, is a way of making visible the systems through which we perceive. By setting up a system and following it rigorously, I create a situation in which the viewer can see the system of perception itself — can see how they are ordering what they see.
