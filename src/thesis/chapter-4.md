## Truth

According to Claude Lévi-Strauss there is no final truth, at least not in science. It is about asking the right questions. I wonder if in my search for similarities in the subjects I choose, or in putting certain things together, comparing, repetition, showing what is there… I am looking for a sort of general truth. And if there is no truth, what then does Claude Lévi-Strauss mean when he says that ‘(…) they [myths] make it possible to discover certain operational modes of the human mind, which have remained so constant over the centuries, and are so widespread over immense geographical distances, that we can assume them to be fundamental and can seek to find them in other societies and in other areas of mental life, where their presence was not suspected, and whose nature is thereby illuminated.’[^1]

He is finding common ground, and by finding it in one area, you can specifically look for it in other areas. This common ground (‘fundamental’) could I think be considered as a truth. On the other hand, maybe I am confusing truth with coherency, recurring or unity. And that this should not be considered as a general rule. It could apply it to most, but not necessarily to all. Just like when he states that ‘music and mythology appeal to mental structures that the different listeners have in common.’[^2] That there are things in common doesn’t mean it is a truth. And indeed, when is it ever possible to say something is a truth? You can never know everything, so there is always the possibility that somewhere there exists a contradiction. Something Lévi-Straus also notices:

(…, the fact still remains that there does not exist, nor ever will exist, any community or group of communities whose mythology and ethnography (…[^3]) can be known in their entirety. The ambition to achieve such knowledge is meaningless, since we are dealing with a shifting reality, perpetually exposed to the attacks of a past that destroys it and of a future that changes it.[^4]

So there’s another ambition I can lay aside. But maybe not in certain moves when making art. I think the perhaps meaningless pursuit of completeness tells a story and can be an important element in an art piece. It is common sense that absolute completeness doesn’t exist, so knowingly going against this tells a story.

Lévi-Strauss almost never talks about a truth. He is saying things about underlying similarities: *‘structuralism (…*[^5]*) reveals, behind phenomena, a unity and coherence that could not be brought out by a simple description of the facts, laid out flat, so to speak, and presented in random order to the enquiring mind.’*[^6]

Something I like very much in the quote is that at one point he is even saying that this unity doesn’t even exist in reality:

*If it has any unity, that unity will appear only behind or beyond the text and, in the best hypothesis, will become a reality in the mind of the reader.*[^7]

I think that is beautiful, and a value I would like to put in art. So I will stick to that there is no truth, or maybe what the Dutch writer Eduard Douwes Dekker wrote: ‘Perhaps nothing is entirely true, not even this!’[^8]


## Unity

So there is something in unity and looking for it. This is an element Lévi-Strauss definitely talks about a lot when he describes his working method and is apparently also a key feature of Structuralism.

*Genuine structuralism, on the contrary [to mathematics], seeks first and foremost to grasp the intrinsic properties of certain types of categories*[^9]

And in the case of Lévi-Strauss his research:

*I discerned a small number of simple principles, thanks to which a very complex mass of customs and practices, at first sight absurd (and generally held to be so), could be reduced to a meaningful system.*[^10]

The unity of subjects, which sometimes have at first sight no similarities in them, is a key element in my art practice. I use it at two different stages. Subjects that have this element in them trigger my interest and become the research subject for a possible work. It won’t always necessary end up being an element of the final work. But in a lot of works it is still visible. The reason for me to investigate the pilgrim’s route from Oslo to Trondheim was that pilgrims didn’t only use this route, but it was also the coronation route. So kings travelled along it, and next to kings, it was the postal route to Trondheim. Kings, pilgrims and postmen were travelling on it, giving this same road three different meanings. Of course roads are used by many different people and for all kinds of reasons. Though while you are travelling on it, it is your road, it is there for you. The fact that this bit of road has a clear route and three clear main characters with different reasons for travel, giving the road different meanings, makes it a good place to show this element.


## System

Claude Lévi-Strauss gives a more elaborate description of his working method in the following quote:

In order to draw my map, I have been obliged to work outward from the center: first I establish the semantic field surrounding a given myth, with the help of ethnography and by means of other myths; and then I repeat the operation in the case of each of these myths. In this way the arbitrarily chosen central zone can be crisscrossed by various intersecting lines, although fewer overlappings occur as we move further out. In order to make the grid or mesh even, one would have to repeat the process several times, by drawing more circles around points situated along the periphery. But at the same time this would increase the size of the original area. And so we see that the analysis of myths is an endless task. Each step forward creates a new hope, realization of which is dependent on the solution of some new difficulty. The evidence is never complete.[^11]

Again a parallel with research for an art project. Every side road you research opens up new inspiration and new roads to follow. The difficulty is when to stop going into side roads and from the whole bulk of information and inspiration you’ve gathered decide what is relevant and what not. What is the element you lift out of it and highlight? Because for me the interest isn’t to show the whole set of connections. I research and try to be as complete as possible in finding the connections in order for me to decide which one or two can make the piece work.

Lévi-Strauss in his description sounds also very much like the definition of a system. In this case a centralized one I think that is moving towards a state of progressive systematization. So it is striving for a state of wholeness, but never getting there, since ‘the evidence is never complete’. A bit earlier on in the text he summarizes it as:

*Thus is brought into being a multidimensional body, whose central parts disclose a structure, while uncertainty and confusion continue to prevail along its periphery.*[^12]

Another part of his study that refers to systems is the sorting of elements in objects and environment[^13], or in his case terms and environments. And just as with systems he runs into the same problem, ‘the problem of arriving at an unequivocal definition of the constituent elements of myths either as terms or as relations; according to the variants being considered and the different stages of the analysis, each term can appear as a relation and each relation as a term.’[^14]

A solution given by the systems theory is to divide the system into smaller parts.[^15] I thought that this would only make the confusion bigger. But another view on this is provided by Lévi-Strauss: *‘It is simplified, and its very poverty reduces it to its essential features.’*[^16]So by making the problem smaller you get to the essence of it. The question, however, of how to reduce still remains. And by reducing the wrong way, you end up with the wrong essence.

Another method is coding works, substituting information for different information. Codes, even though they can be very complicated, in my opinion can make a clear representation of something complex.

The social sciences are no doubt comparable to the physical and natural sciences in the sense that neither achieves direct apprehension of reality, but only of the symbols in terms of which the mind perceives reality in accordance with the constraints and thresholds of our sensory system.[^17]

Moreover, giving objects (elements, units, whatever) a hierarchy will offer insight into the matter at hand. And symbols sound very cold and impersonal. Turner has another view on this:

*The symbols and their relations . . . are not only a set of cognitive classifications for ordering the universe . . . They are also, and perhaps as importantly, a set of evocative devices for arousing, channeling and domesticating powerful emotions such as hatred, fear, affection, grief. They are also informed with purposiveness and have a ‘conative’ aspect. In brief, the whole person and not just the mind . . . is existentially involved in life and death issues (V. W. Turner 3, pp. 42-3)*[^18]


## Aesthetic Enjoyment

With all the references to these serious sciences, systems, symbols, orders etc. the aspect of emotions seems forgotten. But Lévi-Strauss talks about how we perceive the myths, and since he often compares the myths to music he has beautiful descriptions of how we listen to music and what it does to our minds. Especially one about how composers play with our minds:

Aesthetic enjoyment is made up of this multiplicity of excitements and moments of respite, of expectations disappointed or fulfilled beyond anticipation -

a multiplicity resulting from the challenges made by the work and from the contradictory feeling it arouses that the tests it is subjecting us to are impossible, at the same time as it prepares to provide us with the marvelously unpredictable means of coping with them.[^19]

They play with the expectations and anticipations, sometimes giving in to them and other times offering surprise, but they always stay within a certain range so as not to lose their audience. (Don’t drift off too far.) According to Lévi-Strauss, ‘a musical work is a sound system capable of inducing meanings in the mind of the listener.’[^20] I don’t work with sounds, but I do hope to induce meaning in the mind of those who see my work. I think that how he describes the substance of myths is more relevant to my work. And maybe here I even dare to say that this refers to conceptual art.

*The substance of the myth is neither in the style nor in the form of the narrative, nor in the syntax, but in the story that it tells.*[^21]


## Inspiration

Apart from comparing myths with music, Lévi-Strauss also takes painting into consideration. He sees music and myths as very similar but painting as quite a different field. He isn’t comparing painting immediately to myth but to music. It seems he thinks music is the superior art form, but maybe I just get this impression because he talks about it most, and maybe that’s only for the reason that music is more closely related to myths. He sees music as a form of art that relates only to itself. It is totally man-made and refers only back to itself.

*It is because the field of operation of music is cultural that music comes into being, free from those representational links that keep painting in a state of subjection to the world of sense experience and its organization in the form of objects.*[^22]

Painting refers back to the world. It can’t sever itself from the subjective senses. I think he is right, but actually that is exactly the thing I really like about art. I will take what he calls painting a bit broader and apply it to art in general. I think the connection to ‘sense experience’ and ‘representational links’ make art interesting. I don’t believe you can really sever yourself from the world and its impression on you. And with music I think that on the level of sounds he is right that it can only go back on its own history. That you can’t see, for example, bird singing as music[^23]. But in the sense of inspiration there are more sources, and I wonder if he isn’t underestimating this. But when he divides music into three kinds of composers[^24], we can imagine he is referring back to inspiration, and that the telling of stories can’t solely relate to music. But going back to art, ‘colors exist in painting only because of the prior existence of colored objects and beings’[^25]. It is a pleasure when things refer back to the real world. It is where we are living, it is part of us. We can’t see ourselves as so superior that only culture conceived by our minds is the right thing.


## Perception

So how do we see the world? How do we perceive the things surrounding us. We take them in, and here already a transformation takes place. We reconstruct them, and the form also changes by again transforming this into objects in reality. According to Lévi-Strauss we already order and structure when we see and how we store things in our minds.

The eye does not simply photograph visible objects; it codifies their relationships, and what it transmits to the brain is not so much figurative images as a system of binary oppositions between immobility and movement, the presence or absence of colour, movement occurring in one direction rather than in others, a certain type of form differing from other types, and so on.[^26]

We see things by the relation they have to others. As previously stated: systems and structures are everywhere and we see them. The question is if we always realize so. And this brings me to a text by Foucault.

*I am concerned, in short, with a history of resemblance: on what conditions was Classical thought able to reflect relations of similarity or equivalence between things, relations that would provide a foundation and a justification for their words, their classifications, their systems of exchange?*

Michel Foucault,[^27]

Foucault describes his work as a Critical History of Thought. *‘A critical history of thought would be an analysis of the conditions under which certain relations of subject to object are formed or modified.’*[^28]

In 1966 he published the book Les Mots et les choses: Une archéologie des sciences humaines. Foucault actually preferred L’Ordre des Choses for the original French title (this is what is used for the English translation, The Order of Things: An Archaeology of the Human Sciences). But since this had already been used by two structuralist works he changed the title. There are two ordering principles he makes out in the chapter about classifying[^29] that are important for this thesis — the system and the method — and I am using both in my work. The method when I am researching, the system when executing a work. Of course there are exceptions to this, but it applies to my main approach.[^30] I have divided the making of a work into some different stages. I have decided to to go through the text by Foucault and describe where and how it fits.
