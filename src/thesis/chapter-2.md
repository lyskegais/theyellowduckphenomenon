## Burnham

In 1968 Jack Burnham published *Systems Aesthetics* in Artforum. Burnham introduced the systems theory of Ludwig von Bertalanffy to the art world. He saw a fundamental shift happening in art away from the object toward systems.

> The aesthetic system I am proposing is oriented not toward the definition of art, but toward the definition of the aesthetic transaction.[^9]

This essay was a groundbreaking text that tried to define this new kind of art making that he saw appearing in the late 1960s. The shift Burnham described was a shift from an object-oriented to a systems-oriented culture. Not the single isolated artwork but the system, the relations between objects and ideas, becomes the subject of art.

## Conceptual Art / Art-and-Technology

Burnham positioned his ideas within the context of both Conceptual Art and the emerging field of Art-and-Technology. Conceptual Art had already started to question the primacy of the art object. Artists like Sol LeWitt, Lawrence Weiner and Joseph Kosuth had argued that the idea or concept behind a work is more important than the physical realisation. If the idea is there, the work exists, even without being physically made.

Art-and-Technology was a movement that saw art and science as natural partners. Artists worked with engineers and scientists, used new technologies to create works that would not have been possible before. The focus was on process, on systems, on feedback loops.

## Environment

Burnham saw the concept of environment as central to understanding this new art. The environment is not just the physical space in which art is displayed but the entire context — including social and political conditions — in which the work is produced and received. The artwork is always part of a larger system and must be understood in relation to this larger system.

This connects directly to von Bertalanffy's definition of environment: everything that is not part of the system itself but influences it. For Burnham, the art world — galleries, critics, collectors, institutions — is the environment of the artwork. Changing the environment changes what the artwork is and does.

## Conceptual Art

Conceptual Art as a movement was defined by its questioning of the traditional definition of art. If art could be an idea, then the boundaries of what art could be were dramatically expanded. The question became not what something is, but how it functions within a system of relations.

Burnham was sympathetic to Conceptual Art but wanted to go further. For him, even the concept was not enough. What mattered was the system — the total set of relations within which the artwork exists and operates. The concept is just one element in this system.
