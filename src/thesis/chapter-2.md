## Burnham

Jack Burnham (1931) worked as a sculptor until about 1968. In 1960 he had started teaching and this was what he continued to do. He became more an art theorist and critic. Additionally he was a regular contributor to Artforum and curated art shows. In 1968 he wrote an article entitled ‘System Aesthetics’ for Artforum. In this article he tried to explain the art movement that arose at that time, post-formalist art. He used the book by Bertalanffy as inspiration and it was only published in early 1968, although he wrote the first texts on this subject in 1950. Burnham applied the system theory to the art movement that was going on at that moment. It is quite hard to comment and reflect on a movement when you are in the middle of it. The text in its time was picked up mostly by artists. Art historians ignored it until the 1990s and, especially since 2000, the text has witnessed a revival and is used and discussed again

In 2005 there was an exhibition at the Tate Modern in London called Open Systems – Rethinking Art c. 1970. Luke Skrebowski wrote an article based on this exhibition. He wanted to ‘revisit Burnham’s theory and reconsider its value’. In later life Burnham rejected his own system aesthetics and it became a rather unknown theory. Skrebowski argued that by totally rejecting the theory we might have lost a tool for understanding the art made at the end of the 60s and the beginning of the 70s. Besides trying to revive it he also suggested why Burnham’s system aesthetics failed: ‘Burnham hinted at, but never comprehensively followed through on, a disarticulation of systems theory from its techno-industrial deployment. In so doing he only suggested the conceptual possibilities that systems theory might offer a critical art practice. Burnham too often carelessly elided systems theory and cybernetics.’ Burnham might have left out the systems theory and cybernetics from time to time. As I understand it, the problem was that he never really made a theoretically clear distinction between the conceptual art and art-and-technology movement that can be derived from the systems aesthetics

## Conceptual Art/Art-and-Technology

In hindsight it is a lot easier to see the two movements as separate even if they have some similarities. So with the advantage of looking at the movements thirty years after they started, it is very helpful to distinguish them. Edward A. Shanken writes and teaches about the entwinement of art, science, and technology with a focus on interdisciplinary practices involving new media. He has studied Burnham and explains the terminology of conceptual art and art-and-technology in the following way:

Conceptual art has sought to analyze the ideas underlying the creation and reception of art, rather than to elaborate another stylistic convention in the historical succession of modernist avant-garde movements. Investigations by conceptual artists into networks of signification and structures of knowledge (which enable art to have meaning) have frequently employed text as a strategic device to examine the interstice between visual and verbal languages as semiotic systems. In this regard, conceptual art is a meta-critical and self-reflexive art process. It is engaged in theorizing the possibilities of signification in art’s multiple contexts (including its history and criticism, exhibitions and markets). In interrogating the relationship between ideas and art, conceptual art de-emphasizes the value traditionally accorded to the materiality of art objects. It focuses, rather, on examining the preconditions for how meaning emerges in art, seen as a semiotic system

Art-and-technology has focused its inquiry on the materials and/or concepts of technology and science, which it recognizes artists have historically incorporated in their work. Its investigations include: (1) the aesthetic examination of the visual forms of science and technology, (2) the application of science and technology in order to create visual forms and (3) the use of scientific concepts and technological media both to question their prescribed applications and to create new aesthetic models. In this third case, art-and-technology, like conceptual art, is also a meta-critical process. It challenges the systems of knowledge (and the technologically mediated modes of knowing) that structure scientific methods and conventional aesthetic values. Further, it examines the social and aesthetic implications of technological media that define, package and distribute information

So apart from the meta-critical process, they are very different movements and it is essential to keep them apart

It is impossible to divide artists in the one or other movement. Often their works involve both movements. But the description (of what they are/what it involves) of both is different, and even when they can apply both to one artist, it is not the same movement. ‘Burnham illustrated his theory of systems aesthetics by referring to a wide range of artists, including Haacke, Smithson, and Oppenheim, whose work was not associated primarily with tech art but with environmental art.’ So maybe because he took artists who worked inside the two movements to illustrate, the boundaries became a bit blurred. Also, it might not always be such a good thing to try and put artists in a certain corner. ‘The historicization of an artist’s work as conceptual art or art and technology says less about their work than it does about the institutional mechanisms that have created and reinforced categorical distinctions … at the expense of identifying continuities between them.’

## Environment

The similarity between the two movements lies in their dealing with environment, something that stems directly from the systems theory. ‘Environmental artists and tech artists both sought out and engaged in collaborations in which the artist entered into a new relationship with the environment, space, public arena, onto the terrain of other sciences.’ This working with the environment is the element where my interest lies. Environment is something you can’t deny or avoid. Burnham had, I think, the same opinion: ‘The notion that art can be separated from its everyday environment is a cultural fixation, as is the ideal of objectivity in science.’ Next to that, my work often starts with an outside element. Further, the importance of a public in a work and already having this in mind while making it seems to me to be very related to environment. Thinking about public as environment is also about communication. They are part of your environment and you interact with it/them. This is I think communication. Something to keep in mind is that although I do read the newspaper and am not totally ignorant about what is happening in the world, I do wonder how much of it is really in my work as a political something or a reaction to it. I do think that you can’t really deny it. You know what’s going on, so whether you want it or not, it will influence your work. But I usually try to avoid politics because I’m not sure if my opinion on it will really make a difference or is of any interest

Burnham’s theory, although taken from a very technical field, is not meant to merely apply to the art-and-technology movement. This is a mistake often made and apparently partly due to his own fault. The art-and-technology movement is not to my interest. I’m interested in how the systems theory/aesthetics applies to conceptual art, and therefore I am more interested in the theory than the visual implications

## Conceptual Art

Since my focus will be on conceptual art, I think it’s good to give a second description of the movement. Conceptual art is a difficult movement to describe and there are therefore many different definitions. The description that will be most important to me comes from Tony Godfrey

Conceptual art is not about forms or materials, but about ideas and meanings. It cannot be defined in terms of any medium or style, but rather by the way it questions what art is. In particular, Conceptual art challenges the traditional status of the art object as unique, collectable or saleable. Because the work does not take a traditional form it demands a more active response from the viewer, indeed it could be argued that the Conceptual work of art only truly exists in the viewer’s mental participation. This art can take a variety of forms: everyday objects, photographs, maps, videos, charts and especially language itself. Often there will be a combination of such forms
> No, no, artists think the same way chess players think. You know, they have a set number of rules, they make an exhaustive search about probabilities, the way the chessboard is set up. They say: ‘Well I can make a move here, a move here, this is bad, this might be good.’ They push it two or three moves further and they say: ‘Aha, this is what I will do.’ This is what recursion is. And it’s ... it’s really the whole basis of problem solving
>
